## Section 2: How to Spot Phishing Emails (5 min)

Phishing is the #1 way attackers get in. Here are three real patterns to watch for:

### Example 1: The Fake Invoice

> Subject: "Invoice #8831 — Payment Overdue"
> From: accounts@[misspelled-vendor-name].com

**Red flags:** Unexpected invoice. Sender domain doesn't match the real vendor. Attachment you weren't expecting.

### Example 2: The Urgent Password Reset

> Subject: "Your account will be locked in 24 hours"
> Link goes to: micr0soft-login.com (not microsoft.com)

**Red flags:** Creates urgency. Link URL doesn't match the real service. You didn't request a reset.

### Example 3: The Boss Request

> Subject: "Quick favour — don't tell anyone"
> From: [CEO first name]@gmail.com (not their work email)

**Red flags:** Unusual request. Asks for secrecy. Sent from a personal email, not their work account.

**The rule:** When in doubt, don't click. Ask [IT CONTACT NAME] or your manager first.

> **Presenter note:** If possible, show these on screen. Ask the group: "What looks wrong here?" before revealing the red flags.

## Section 4: What to Do If You Clicked Something Suspicious (3 min)

It happens. Don't panic, and don't try to hide it. Speed matters more than blame.

### Immediately:

1. **Disconnect** from Wi-Fi or unplug your network cable (don't turn off the computer).
2. **Call** [IT CONTACT NAME] at [IT CONTACT EMAIL/PHONE].
3. **Don't** try to fix it yourself, delete files, or run antivirus on your own.

### After you've reported it:

4. Write down what happened: what you clicked, when, and what you saw.
5. Change passwords for any accounts you entered credentials into.

**Remember:** Reporting fast is what protects the company. You will never be punished for reporting a mistake.

> **Presenter note:** Say this part clearly and sincerely. People won't report if they fear blame.
